Reimagining Mental Health Intake for Trauma-Informed Care

Designing a service experience that reduces barriers to mental health care while centering the dignity and agency of people seeking support in their most vulnerable moments.

Overview

The current mental health intake process is broken

Many people seeking mental health support drop out before their first appointment. The intake process—designed around administrative needs rather than human needs—creates additional trauma at a moment when people are already vulnerable.

The problem: Current intake processes require extensive paperwork, long wait times, repeated disclosure of traumatic experiences to different staff members, and navigation of complex insurance requirements—all before someone can access care.

Design Challenge

How might we redesign the mental health intake experience to reduce barriers to care, minimize re-traumatization, and empower people to access support on their own terms?

Key Research Insights

🔄 Repetition creates re-traumatization

Patients were asked to share their story an average of 3-4 times to different staff members, each time reliving difficult experiences. This wasn't just inefficient—it was harmful.

"By the time I got to my actual therapist, I was exhausted from explaining everything so many times. I almost didn't come back."

⏰ Timing matters more than we think

People seek mental health support in moments of crisis. Long intake processes (2+ weeks) meant that by the time they got care, the crisis had either passed or escalated.

"When I finally called for help, I needed help then. Two weeks later, I'd already found a way to cope—or not cope."

📝 Administrative language creates barriers

Forms used clinical and insurance terminology that many people couldn't understand. This created anxiety and confusion at a moment when clarity was most needed.

"I didn't know what half the questions were asking. I felt stupid, and I was already feeling vulnerable."

🤝 Staff feel the broken system too

Intake coordinators knew the process was flawed but felt constrained by insurance requirements, liability concerns, and legacy systems. They wanted to help but felt stuck.

"I can see their frustration. I wish I could just get them to a therapist faster, but my hands are tied by the process."

03

Ideation

Co-designing solutions with the people who know best

I facilitated three co-design workshops with patients, staff, and clinical leadership. Using trauma-informed facilitation techniques from my social work practice, I created a safe space where everyone could contribute authentically.

The key was framing the challenge not as "fix the form" but as "design for dignity." This reframe helped participants think beyond incremental improvements to imagine fundamentally different approaches.

04

Solution

A trauma-informed intake service ecosystem

Rather than designing a single solution, I created a service blueprint that reimagines the entire intake experience across multiple touchpoints. The solution focuses on three core principles: reduce cognitive load, preserve agency, and minimize re-traumatization.

1

Progressive Intake

Instead of one exhausting session, information is collected gradually across touchpoints. Patients only share what's needed at each stage.

2

Choice Architecture

Patients choose how, when, and where they want to engage—phone, text, in-person, or online. They control the pace.

3

Warm Handoffs

A single "care navigator" guides patients through the entire process, eliminating the need to re-tell their story multiple times.

4

Plain Language Forms

All materials rewritten at 6th-8th grade reading level with clear explanations and examples. No clinical jargon.

5

Transparent Timelines

Clear communication about wait times, next steps, and what to expect. No uncertainty about the process.

6

Bridge Support

While waiting for first appointment, patients receive resources, self-care guidance, and peer support access.

05

Validation

Testing with real people in real contexts

I worked with the clinic to pilot key elements of the redesigned service with 25 new patients over 6 weeks. This wasn't a full implementation, but it allowed us to test the most critical changes and gather feedback.

What we tested:

  • Rewritten intake forms in plain language
  • Progressive disclosure (split forms into smaller chunks)
  • Single care navigator model for continuity
  • Choice of intake channels (phone, online, in-person)
  • Bridge resources while waiting for first appointment

Feedback-driven iterations:

Based on patient feedback, we made several adjustments during the pilot. For example, we added more visual progress indicators so people knew how much was left. We also simplified our "choice" structure after learning that too many options felt overwhelming.

Impact

Beyond the numbers: real human impact

While the quantitative results were promising, the qualitative feedback showed the true value of this work. Patients reported feeling "heard," "respected," and "less overwhelmed." Staff felt more aligned with their mission to help people.

"This felt like someone actually cared about making it easier for me. I didn't feel like just another file." — Pilot participant

"For the first time, I felt like I could do my job the way I wanted to—actually helping people instead of just processing paperwork." — Intake coordinator

Next Steps for Implementation

The clinic is now working to secure funding for full implementation. Key next steps include:

  • Training all staff on trauma-informed intake practices
  • Integrating redesigned forms into electronic health record system
  • Hiring dedicated care navigators
  • Developing bridge support resources and peer support program
  • Creating measurement framework to track long-term impact
07

Reflection

What I Learned

Service design is about changing systems, not just experiences. This project reinforced that real change requires addressing not just the patient-facing experience but also the underlying policies, workflows, and incentives that shape that experience.

My social work background was my superpower. I could facilitate sensitive conversations, understand trauma-informed principles, and navigate the complex healthcare ecosystem in ways that purely trained designers might struggle with. This validated my decision to bring my full background into design work.

Co-design is powerful but requires careful facilitation. Creating space for people to share painful experiences while also generating ideas required thoughtful structure and emotional intelligence. Not every workshop methodology works in these contexts.

If I Could Do It Again

I would have engaged IT and compliance stakeholders earlier in the process. Some of our best ideas faced technical and regulatory hurdles that could have been addressed sooner with their input.

I also would have pushed harder to include more diverse voices in the research phase, particularly people from marginalized communities who face additional barriers to mental health care. Their experiences would have made the solution even stronger.
